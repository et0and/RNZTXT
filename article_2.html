Japan earthquake death toll rises over 160

The death toll from Japan's devastating New Year's Day earthquake has risen to 161, authorities say.

By Kathryn Armstrong, BBC News

A damaged car lies underneath a collapsed building in Shika town, Ishikawa Prefecture on 8 January. Photo: RNZ/ Philip Fong

The death toll from Japan's devastating New Year's Day earthquake has risen to 161, authorities say.

Efforts are continuing in attempts to find more than 100 people who remain missing a week later.

But bad weather is hampering rescuers - with heavy rain and snow triggering warnings of landslides and building collapses.

The 7.6 magnitude earthquake struck the remote Noto peninsula - toppling buildings and sparking a major fire.

The majority of the deaths have happened in the hard-hit cities of Wajima and Suzu.

Meanwhile, the number of people missing has dropped from 195 to just over 100. The death toll has jumped from the 120 reported on Sunday.

Police officers check a collapsed building in the city of Wajima, on 5 January. Photo: AFP/ Toshifumi Kitamura

More than 2000 people are reportedly still cut off due to massive damage to roads. Several others are living in emergency shelters.

The Japanese military has been handing out supplies including food, water and blankets for those who have had to vacate their homes.

The defence ministry said in a statement on Sunday that it had sent nearly 6000 troops out to help with the relief mission.

They added that they believe there are still people alive who need rescue and vowed to continue their lifesaving operations despite the ending of a critical 72 hour window to find survivors.

A man walks past the ruins of a shopping district in the city of Wajima, on 4 January. Photo: AFP/ Kazuhiro Nogi

Stories of miraculous recoveries have been emerging. A woman in her 90s was found alive under rubble five days after the earthquake hit, in Suzu.

Those in the worst-affected areas are being urged to keep their guard up as they continue to experience further quakes.

As of early Monday local time, more than 1200 tremors had been recorded since New Year's Day, Japanese public broadcaster NHK reported.

Japan is one of the most seismically active countries in the world, and activity has been increasing in the Noto area since the end of 2020.

More than 500 small and medium earthquakes have hit there over the past three years.
