Relics found buried in coffee jars under Christchurch cathedral

Parts of history found buried under a cathedral in coffee jars will see the light of day again in a Christchurch exhibition this week.

A box containing relics recovered during deconstruction of the Cathedral of the Blessed Sacrament in April 2021. Photo: Supplied / Catholic Diocese of Christchurch

Parts of history found buried under a cathedral in coffee jars will see the light of day again in a Christchurch exhibition this week.

Father Kevin entombed the Catholic relics under the side chapel of the Cathedral of the Blessed Sacrament nearly 50 years ago, in 1975.

They were unearthed again after the Canterbury Earthquakes, when the cathedral was demolished.

Christchurch diocese archivist Triona Doocey said why he chose coffee jars and other pantry containers as the vessels remains a mystery.

"It was just a really practical solution because you need something that's air tight, water tight, that's going to protect them and the jars did the job. There was one other jar that did leak, and that was a jam jar rather than a coffee jar. So if you're doing a time capsule, do not use jam jars," she said.

"Why go to the expense of having something fancy made when the jars did what they were supposed to do? There was also a Schweppes bottle, which, I think it was a bit of a joke on Father Kevin's part - he had lottery tickets rolled up inside it.

"That was quite entertaining to open that and see he had a good sense of humour," Doocey said.

The Cathedral of the Blessed Sacrament before it was demolished. Photo: Supplied / Catholic Diocese of Christchurch

It was also unclear why Father Kevin decided to bury the relics in the first place, she said.

"We think possibly it might be that relics became almost uncool. So he put them in a steel box, what we call a tabernacle, and he buried them beneath the side chapel and he'd written a note to say they were buried...it was just a matter of finding them."

The relics were collected by the first bishop of Christchurch, Bishop Grimes, who held the position from 1887-1915.

He picked them up on trips to the Vatican, and had an extensive collection - some are believed to date back to the medieval times, Doocey said.

"He was on the opposite side of the world to the Vatican, to Rome, to everybody's home who were here in the newly established city and I think he wanted to provide a little bit of a connection for them," she said.

"Everybody had their favourite saints, so he really did have a widespread selection."
