Pacific countries struggling to keep up with drug syndicates

Drug syndicates are quickly evolving and Pacific law enforcement is struggling to keep up, an expert says.

Fiji meth bust. Photo: Fiji Police

Drug syndicates evolve at a speed Pacific law enforcement is struggling to keep up with, the director of a geopolitical consultancy agency says.

Fiji police have seized three-and-a-half tonnes of methamphetamine, in what they say is one of the largest seizures seen in the country.

Local police have not put a street value on the drugs, but based on the NZ Drug Trends Survey it could be priced in the hundreds of millions of dollars if it landed here.

Strategika Group advises law enforcement agencies in the Pacific and its managing director Jose Sousa-Santos said there was a capacity issue with Pacific law enforcement.

"The problem here is that these types of syndicates evolve at a speed at which law enforcement in the Pacific is struggling to keep up with," he told RNZ Morning Report.

"It's an issue of capacity, it's an issue of cooperation, and I think law enforcement - not just in the Pacific, but Australia [and] New Zealand - need to have stronger ties with their counterparts in South America and in Southeast Asia to ensure that the response is holistic.

"You cannot beat a machine such as the syndicates or the cartels, linked to Australian criminal organised crime or New Zealand criminal organised crime, without a cohesive strategy behind it."

The origin of the seized drugs was not yet known, but Sousa-Santos said the "worst-case scenario" was if it was produced in Fiji aimed for export.

"I'm not going to speculate. But definitely the amount would indicate that it would be for export.

"It's been quite a few years since we've seen a seizure of this size of methamphetamines. We have seen seizures around the size of cocaine in the Pacific over the past several years."

Acting Police Commissioner Juki Fong Chew said Fiji's "fight against drugs" continues in the form of "consistent raids" with "more drugs being seized".

"This is one of the biggest seizures ever and this shows that we are on top of things," he said.

Last year, the Fijian government admitted it was losing the fight against the sale and consumption of illegal drugs.

Attorney-General Siromi Turaga pleaded with traditional leaders to work with police to address the drug problem, local media reported.
