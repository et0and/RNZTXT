Perth teen shot dead by police after car park stabbing

The teenager had been radicalised online and was part of a programme designed to counter violent extremism.

Perth law enforcement officials address media. Photo: Screenshot / ABC

A major investigation is underway in Perth after police shot dead a teenager who stabbed a member of the public.

The 16-year-old boy had been known to police and was part of a programme designed to help individuals at risk of being radicalised, the ABC reported.

The incident unfolded in a Bunnings car park in Willetton, in the city's south, overnight.

At a press conference on Sunday, Police Commissioner Col Blanch said police received an emergency call just after 10pm on Saturday, from a teen who said he was going to commit "acts of violence".

Members of Western Australia's Muslim community, who had received messages from the teen and were "concerned" by his behaviour, also contacted police prior to the shooting, the ABC reported.

"Our police responded within minutes," Blanch said.

The teen was shot by an officer after he rushed at police with a kitchen knife.

A man was found later with stab wounds to his back. It was believed one could have punctured a lung, the ABC reported.

The man was recovering in hospital and was in a serious but stable condition on Sunday.

Blanch said the teenager had "complex issues, both mental but also online radicalisation issues".

He was known to police, but it was believed that he acted alone.

Blanch said the teen was part of a support programme for people with concerning ideologies, who "may not be committing any crimes".

The stabbing was not being labelled a terror attack at this time, the ABC reported.

Police were working with the boy's family, who were being "very forthcoming", Blanch said.

"You know, I think this is a very challenged individual who was there on the night.

"I think the family really do understand the situation that police were in."

Police Minister Paul Papalia said the fact that the teenager was in the Countering Violent Extremism programme showed how challenging it was to "de-radicalise" people.

"It is a really tough task and none of the programs that have been attempted anywhere have been universally successful," he told the ABC.

"[It is] a really serious challenge in changing someone who's been radicalised and attempting them to get back into a more reasonable pathway.

"This individual may not have done anything that would have enabled police to do anything other than get him into this programme."

WA Premier Roger Cook said he had called a meeting of multi-faith community leaders and would attend that later on Sunday.
