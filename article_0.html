Watch live: Efeso Collins remembered as family's 'superstar' at funeral

The Green Party MP was "my mum and dad's sunshine", always helping and supporting others, his sister says. Video

The late Green Party MP Fa'anānā Efeso Collins has been remembered as his family's "superstar" at a service in South Auckland.

The 49-year-old husband and father-of-two died on 21 February after collapsing during a charity even in Auckland's central city.

Fa'anānā's unexpected death came as a shock to many, with his aiga - including wife Fia and daughters Kaperiela and Asalemo - saying he was "the anchor of our tight-knit family".

Politicians and members of the public, including school students, are among those attending Fa'anānā's funeral at Due Drop Event Centre in Manukau from 1pm on Thursday.

Many of the guests are dressed in traditional Pacific clothing, and a gospel choir sang as the crowd filled the room.

Green Party co-leaders Marama Davidson and James Shaw and Labour leader Chris Hipkins in the crowd at Fa'anānā Efeso Collins' funeral. Photo: RNZ/Nick Monro

To start the service, poet Karlo Mila read a poem that finished: "You become the ancestor we always knew you were."

Family spokesman Taito Eddie Tuiavii then gave a formal greeting in Samoan, paying tribute to Fa'anānā and his villages.

He described Fa'anānā as "larger than life".

It was an "indescribable feeling" to mourn the loss of "our champion", Tuiavii said.

"His death spanned social, political, cultural, religious, ethnic and geographical boundaries."

Photo: RNZ/Nick Monro

Reverend Unasa Uesifili Unasa said a prayer, asking God: "Give us clarity of mind and singleness of heart."

The crowd was then asked to stand to sing a praise song, accompanied by guitars.

Those without good singing voices were told: "Be like Efeso, pick whatever key you want and go for gold."

A Bible reading followed, covering the story of the transfiguration - the moment that Jesus revealed his divine glory to some of his disciplines - from the Gospel of Mark.

Fa'anānā's sisters then took the stage to share stories from his life.

As a child, Fa'anānā was known as 'Boppa', his sister Jemima said. Growing up in Ōtara, he loved playing and watching cricket.

Mourners embrace at the Due Drop Events Centre. Photo: RNZ/Nick Monro

"We didn't have much growing up in Ōtara, but we were raised with an abundance of love, and that made us pretty rich."

Fa'anānā preferred watching the TV news to children's programmes and loved trivia.

He attended Auckland Grammar School for just two weeks, before deciding to leave due to "racist comments", his sister said. He then transferred to "the mighty" Tangaroa College before going on to Auckland University.

Fa'anānā always had "a deep friendship with Jesus", the crowd heard.

"Efeso was able to reach so many people because of his relationship with Jesus."

Jemima signed off by saying: "Manuia lau malaga (rest in peace), Boppa. Until we meet in the clouds."

Another of Fa'anānā's sisters, Millie Collins, described her brother as "our family's golden boy".

Prime Minister Christopher Luxon. Photo: RNZ/Nick Monro

"He was my mum and dad's sunshine, and to his brothers and sisters, his cousins and friends, he was our superstar."

He was always helping out his extended family, whether that be financially, emotionally or by running errands, she said.

Fa'anānā was "one of a kind", Millie Collins said.

"[He was] born to impact the world, born to lead through service. A visionary, a loving, honorable son, husband, father, brother, cousin, nephew and friend."

Samoan singer-songwriter Annie Grace followed the sisters' tributes with the hymn It Is Well With My Soul, performed in both English and Samoan.

Dickie Humphries, who has known Fa'anānā since they attended Auckland University, addressed his friend's widow directly, saying he was heartbroken that they had been parted.

"He wanted to love you through a long life," he told Fia.

However, he was also happy Fa'anānā had found "his best friend, his greatest champion", he said.

Fa'anānā's legacy had showed him "we must live big lives", Humphries said.

Te Pāti Māori co-leaders Debbie Ngarewa-Packer and Rawiri Waititi. Photo: RNZ/Nick Monro

"Lives of service, lives that leave this world better for having been in it. Lives that make right on the legacy of Efeso."

He said all gathered there must keep working towards a better Aotearoa - one where Pasifika people did not die young, or face racist abuse while in Parliament.

Humphries remembered his friend as someone with "an inquiring mind and a curious heart".

"He had an unwavering belief in the brilliance of our people. There are many of us here today who can credit Efeso with a pivotal moment in our lives."

Among the people at the funeral are Green Party co-leaders Marama Davidson and James Shaw, Prime Minister Christopher Luxon, Labour leader Chris Hipkins, and National's Gerry Brownlee, Auckland Mayor Wayne Brown, Te Pāti Māori co-leaders Debbie Ngarewa-Packer and Rawiri Waititi.

Fa'anānā's wife and daughters are wearing the dresses they wore at Parliament earlier this month, when Fa'anānā gave his maiden speech as an MP.
