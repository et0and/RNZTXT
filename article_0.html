New York shaken by rare 4.8-magnitude earthquake

The quake, the area's most significant in decades, grounded flights at major airports.

New York and other parts of the east coast were shaken by a 4.8-magnitude earthquake. Photo: 123RF

New York has been rattled by an earthquake which was felt by millions on the United States' east coast.

The 4.8-magnitude shake was the most significant earthquake in the area in decades.

The quake shut down air traffic at JFK Airport in New York and Newark International in New Jersey.

All flights had since resumed, the Federal Aviation Administration told BBC News.

The epicentre was in Lebanon, New Jersey, about 64km from New York City.

Residents described their homes trembling and furniture shaking.

New York governor Kathy Hochul said no "life-threatening situations" had been reported, but warned residents of potential aftershocks.

New Jersey resident Sally Osman told BBC News she thought the shaking was just intense wind at first.

"We've had early bad weather so I thought it was just wind that was picking up really strong."

Then Osman felt a sudden strong shake.

"I thought a Mack truck hit my home."

When the shaking did not stop, she realised it was an earthquake. Osman grabbed her daughters and stood under a door frame.

"It was pretty intense," she told BBC News.

"I don't even know how long it was but it felt eternal."

There were reports of cracks in footpaths and roads.

Schools had been told to operate as normal following the quake.
